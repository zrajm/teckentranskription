SSLWrite – Adapting ASLWrite for SSL

Background

This is an attempt at adapting ASLWrite for writing Swedish Sign Language (SSL). This attempt is not ’official’ or ’endorsed’ in any way (whatever that would mean), but is simply my own stab at creating a writing system for a language I'm learning, in order to facilitate my own learning.

I am a (hearing) student of SSL. As of May 2018 I'm just finishing the second semester of the sign language interpreter program at Stockholm University. This attempt at adapting ASLWrite for SSL was born out of my desire to be able take notes, and to improve my language skills by reading (reading lots of books – have been an immensely important factor in learning syntax and in increasing my vocabulary in all languages I've studied so far – though in this case I'll have to write those books myself).

My own interest in sign language was initially sparked by an encounter with the transcription system commonly used in sign language research here in Sweden (it looks like this: 􌤴􌥗􌥃􌥃􌤴􌤶􌤟􌥰􌦋􌥠􌤢􌥔􌤸􌤩􌤴􌥙􌥣􌤟􌥻). I have since worked with The Swedish Sign Language Lexicon to develop a typeface (FreeSans-SWL) for that transcription system.

But, for various reasons[1], I don't think that that transcription system is suitable for everyday use.

Handshapes

In ASLWrite nomenclature the individual handshape glyphs are called ‘digits’ and the whole collection of them is known as ‘the digibet’.

Even though there is much overlap between the American and the Swedish Sign Languages, both languages have a number of features that are particular to them. The following table is an attempt at creating all the digits necessary for writing SSL.

Different PDF documents found on the ASLWrite website have different versions of the ASLWrite digibet (presumably reflecting different development stages of the writing system). In order to make sense of them I've here tried to document all the differing versions I've found.

I've found Jolanta Lapiak’s handshape charts (some of them listing experimental digits) a good source of inspiration for coming up with the missing digits for SSL. (I'm not 100% certain, but these seem to be older ideas, experiments and revisions intended to be used by the ASLWrite and si5s writing systems.)

1 Hand Description: Each hand consists of five finger description in the order thumb–index–long–ring–little finger. The following letters are used to indicate finger shape.

  • b = Böjt/bent – Finger/thumb angled in same way as v but all joints are slightly bent giving finger/thumb a rounded appearance.
  • f = fyrkant/square – Like k, but innermost joint is not bent.
  • k = knutet/fisted – The finger is maximally bent, in the same way it would be if hand was turned into a fist.
  • r = rak/straight finger – The finger is completely straight (with no bent joints) and finger/thumb is parallel to the palm.
  • v = vinklat/angled – Finger is bent in the innermost joint, but otherwise straight or thumb is angled up from palm.

If the thumb touches other fingers, then thumb + relevant finger cluster is capitalized (finger cluster = some fingers which are in contact with each other). If thumb is annotated as both separated from (’-’) AND touching the index finger then contact is made at the tip only, if touching without separation then there's contact along the full length of the thumb.

The following symbols are used between thumb/fingers:

  • - = Spread/separated – These fingers do not touch.
  • + = Crossed (can also come before the thumb to indicate that thumb cross other finger, rather than touch at tip).
  • | = Separated by thumb.

Footnotes

[1] On the whole this transcription system for Swedish Sign Language is pretty good. (Yeah, sure, certain things are a little bit awkward – like the fact that the single hand movement in the word ’ball’ require five distinct symbols [􌤴􌥙􌤣􌤣􌤴􌥙􌤟􌥼􌥧􌥯􌦉􌥲􌥿􌥼􌤟].) But it is pretty flexible, and some excellent artistry has been executed in it – like Lars Wallin’s idea of writing polysynthetic signs from the inside out (with the articulation of the non-dominant hand being written backwards in front of the hand configuration).

My main gripe with the system is that it just isn't pretty enough. It wasn't designed with readability in mind: in a larger chunk of text (see example to the right) it is hard to see where one word ends and the next begins, and the abundance of straight lines ruin all chances of easily recognizable bouma.
